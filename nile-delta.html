Nile Delta

Published on 10 April 2024
The Nile River is recognized as one of the world's longest rivers and has served as Egypt's lifeline for thousands of years. North of Cairo the Nile enters the delta region, a level triangular and fertile lowland. The delta was created by sediment deposits, it is a crucial centre for agricultural and sustains a large portion of Egypt's population (Hurst et al., 2024; Scown et al., 2023; Zeydan, 2006).

The existence and growth of the city of Cairo are deeply rooted in the river's history and cultural significance (AlSayyad & Abu-Lughod, 2024). Alexandria, another major city in Egypt, is known for its historical grandeur and strategic location on the Mediterranean coast. Founded by Alexander the Great in 332 BCE, Alexandria was once a centre of Hellenic scholarship and science, and served as the capital of Egypt until 642 CE (Reimer et al., 2024).

Despite the water that has made the region so vital for sustaining life and agriculture, the Nile Delta is now facing a multitude of challenges that threaten its future (Haars et al., 2016; IPCC, 2022; D. J. Stanley & Warne, 1998).

Water Scarcity in the Nile Delta

Among the challenges threatening the Nile Delta, water scarcity stands out as a particularly complex issue. Factors such as rapid population growth and urbanization have escalated the demand for (unpolluted) water (Abdelhafez et al., 2020; Omran, 2017; Wahba et al., 2005). This is particularly evident in densely populated cities like Cairo and Alexandria. Concurrently, inefficient irrigation practices and outdated infrastructure contribute to substantial water loss (Abd-Elmabod et al., 2019). Climate change further compounds the problem, with increased temperatures leading to higher evaporation rates (Siam & Eltahir, 2017). Additionally, upstream activities such as damming and water diversions have diminished the Nile's flow, impacting the volume of water that reaches the delta (Redeker & Kantoush, 2014).

Effects and Measures of Water Scarcity

Water scarcity in the Nile Delta has far-reaching effects, impacting sectors like agriculture, public health, and socio-economic stability. A decrease in water availability can result in crop failure, food insecurity, and heightened competition for resources (Radwan et al., 2019). To address this, measures such as enhancing irrigation efficiency, advocating for water conservation, and investigating alternative water sources like desalination are being pursued (Sallam, 2018; Tawfik et al., 2024). Policy modifications and international collaboration are also integral to sustainable water management in the region.

Land Subsidence in the Nile Delta

Land subsidence, or the sinking of land, is a significant concern in the Nile Delta. The primary cause of this phenomenon is the extraction of groundwater, which leads to land compaction and subsequent sinking (Bucx et al., 2014; J.-D. Stanley & Clemente, 2017). Land subsidence, coupled with sea-level rise due to climate change, heightens the risk of flooding and saltwater intrusion into freshwater resources (Omran, 2017; Sefelnasr & Sherif, 2014). Saltwater intrusion can degrade soil quality and render agricultural land infertile, jeopardizing the livelihoods of farming communities (Tully et al., 2019). This is particularly concerning for cities like Alexandria, which are located on the coast and heavily reliant on the delta's resources (Edmonds et al., 2020). Mitigation efforts include regulating groundwater extraction and implementing strategies to adapt to sea-level rise, such as the construction of sea walls and the development of salt-tolerant crops (A. El-Masry, 2022; Abd-Elhamid et al., 2016; Tully et al., 2019).

Flooding in the Nile Delta

Flooding poses a significant threat to the Nile Delta, particularly due to the combined effects of land subsidence and sea-level rise (Hereher, 2010; Hzami et al., 2021). This is especially problematic for coastal cities like Alexandria, which face a heightened risk of flooding. Historically, the annual flooding of the Nile River was a natural and beneficial event, depositing nutrient-rich silt onto the delta's farmlands (Tawfik et al., 2024; Zeydan, 2006). However, the construction of the Aswan High Dam has significantly curtailed these floods, altering the delta's natural dynamics. Presently, the delta faces the risk of flooding from rising sea levels and the increased intensity and frequency of storm events (Hzami et al., 2021). Sea level rise could submerge 19% of the delta area and affect 6.1 million people, (Eldeberky, 2011). Effective adaptation and mitigation strategies are therefore crucial.
