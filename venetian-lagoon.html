Venetian Lagoon

Published on 10 April 2024
Venice, located on the coast of northeast Italy, is an architectural marvel with a rich history. The city of Venice, founded in the 5th century AD, is spread over 118 small islands (Cessi et al., 2024; UNESCO World Heritage Centre, n.d.). Originally formed by the interaction of Adriatic tidal currents and the waters of several Alpine rivers, the lagoon has always been a crucial factor in the history of Venice (Cessi et al., 2024).

Situated in the heart of a lagoon, Venice occupied a unique position within the Adriatic Sea, in proximity to the Byzantine Empire and providing access to traders from the Near East, which made it an ideal hub for commerce (UNESCO, n.d.). Consequently Venice became a cosmopolitan city and major power in the medieval and early modern world. Nowadays Venice is renowned for its unique network of canals, historic landmarks and architecture, making it a popular destination for tourists from around the world (UNESCO World Heritage Centre, n.d.).

Acqua Alta

While Venice's unique geographical location and architectural beauty have made it a world-renowned tourist destination, it also faces significant natural challenges. One of the most pressing of these is the phenomenon known as Acqua Alta (Caroli & Soriani, 2017; Faranda et al., 2023; Meyer, 2021). Acqua Alta, or "high water," is a phenomenon that occurs in Venice when high tides combine with low air pressures and winds from the southeast, causing the waters of the lagoon to rise and flood the city.

On November 12, 2019 Venice was submerged under the most exceptionally high tide in 50 years. The flooding was recorded as the second highest in the city's history, after the disastrous flood of 1966, also known as the “Great Flood of Venice" (Faranda et al., 2023; Lionello et al., 2021).

As part of a broader plan to protect Venice and its lagoon, construction works of the MOSE project (MOdulo Sperimentale Elettromeccanico, or “Experimental Electromechanical Module”) began in 2003 (Lionello et al., 2021). This storm surge barrier is specifically designed to close off the three inlets connecting the lagoon and the Adriatic Sea during high tides, effectively blocking seawater inflow (Cavaleri et al., 2020; Cavallaro et al., 2017).

Land Subsidence in Venice and the Lagoon

In addition to the rising sea levels discussed earlier, land subsidence in Venice and the Lagoon also poses a significant threat. This phenomenon exacerbates the city's vulnerability to flooding and indicates that Venice is, quite literally, sinking (Caroli & Soriani, 2017; Lionello et al., 2021; Mikhailova, 2021). Land subsidence, both geological and anthropogenic, has played a significant role in the origin and evolution of the lagoon. Geological land subsidence contributed to the origin and evolution of the lagoon, while anthropogenic subsidence has recently assumed major importance for the Venetian environment. In the last century, about 25 cm of subsidence occurred, mainly due to groundwater pumping in the nearby industrial area (Tosi et al., 2013). The subsidence of Venice is not so much renowned for the extent of the ground movement, but rather for the serious threat it poses to the city's heritage and safety, given its minimal elevation above sea level (Lio, C. et al., 2017).

Low tide and water scarcity

In contrast to the issues of sinking and flooding, Venice faces unique challenges during periods of low water levels, leading to dried up canals at low tide. The city's iconic gondolas and vaporetto water buses, which serve as the primary means of transportation, can find their movement hindered during these times. This not only disrupts daily commutes but also impacts the vital tourism industry that the city's economy heavily relies on. Furthermore, emergency services such as ambulance boats can also be affected (Buckley, 2023; Reuters, 2023). To address this, Venice has developed strategies to reroute transportation services during these periods.

In conclusion, Venice's unique geographical characteristics present a complex array of environmental challenges. From managing the impacts of regular floodings to climate extremes and from land subsidence to navigating the issues of low tides and water scarcity, the city continues to adapt and innovate. Looking towards the future, Venice's ongoing efforts in environmental management and sustainable practices will be crucial in preserving its rich cultural heritage and unique urban landscape.
